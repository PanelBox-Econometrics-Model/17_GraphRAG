\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has become the dominant paradigm for grounding large language model responses in authoritative documents. However, conventional vector-based RAG (VectorRAG) fails to meet the requirements of financial document processing: it lacks multi-hop reasoning capability, provides insufficient source attribution, and incurs high token costs when processing large regulatory corpora. We propose a multi-strategy GraphRAG architecture that combines a domain-specific financial ontology with a retrieval engine fusing three parallel strategies---graph traversal, vector similarity, and community summarization---weighted by query intent across six types. Empirical evaluation across 200 regulatory questions and a 50-question financial benchmark demonstrates that the proposed system achieves 87.4\% faithfulness (versus 72.9\% for VectorRAG), 96.4\% source attribution, and 70\% token reduction compared to full-document retrieval. An ablation study confirms that all three retrieval strategies are complementary, with graph retrieval contributing 16.1 percentage points, vector retrieval 9.5 pp, and community retrieval 4.7 pp to the overall benchmark score. We provide practical guidelines for when GraphRAG outperforms VectorRAG and when VectorRAG may suffice.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have rapidly transformed information processing across industries. In the financial sector, banks, insurance companies, and regulatory bodies increasingly deploy LLM-based systems for regulatory compliance verification, financial report analysis, and risk assessment \citep{pwc2024graphllm}. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as the dominant paradigm for grounding LLM responses in authoritative documents, mitigating hallucination by retrieving relevant passages before generating responses \citep{lewis2020rag}.

However, conventional vector-based RAG (hereafter VectorRAG) fails systematically in the financial context for three key reasons. First, \textbf{multi-hop reasoning is mandatory}: financial queries frequently require traversing chains of entity relationships (e.g., determining which subsidiaries have risk exposure under Basel~III), and VectorRAG retrieves semantically similar chunks in isolation without connecting these relational chains \citep{han2025ragvsgraphrag}. Second, \textbf{source attribution is legally required}: every compliance response must cite specific regulatory articles, and VectorRAG returns chunks that are semantically similar but frequently from incorrect sections, undermining traceability. Third, \textbf{token costs are prohibitive}: financial documents are characteristically long (a typical SEC 10-K contains $\sim$80,000 tokens), making full-document retrieval economically infeasible at scale.

GraphRAG---the integration of knowledge graphs into the RAG pipeline---has emerged as a promising approach to address these limitations. The foundational work by \citet{edge2024graphrag} demonstrated that graph-based indexing with community detection improves comprehensiveness for global sensemaking queries. Subsequent work has explored GraphRAG for financial applications \citep{sarmah2024hybridrag, arun2025finreflectkg, graphcompliance2025} and token efficiency \citep{terag2025, lazygraphrag2025, polyg2025}. However, no existing work jointly provides (1)~a financial domain ontology, (2)~multi-strategy retrieval with intent-aware fusion, and (3)~token economy optimization---validated with a rigorous empirical comparison against VectorRAG.

This paper makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multi-strategy retrieval with intent-aware fusion.} We propose an architecture that executes three retrieval strategies in parallel---graph traversal, vector similarity, and community summarization---and fuses results using weights adapted to the query intent across six types. This achieves 87.4\% faithfulness, a 14.5 percentage point (pp) improvement over VectorRAG.

    \item \textbf{Token economy framework.} Structured graph retrieval achieves 70\% token reduction compared to full-document retrieval and 33\% reduction compared to VectorRAG, with batch processing providing additional 50\% indexation cost savings.

    \item \textbf{Financial domain ontology.} A formal ontology with 10 entity types and 14 relationship types, validated against regulatory documents (Basel~III, LGPD, GDPR) and SEC filings, with three-level entity resolution.

    \item \textbf{Empirical evaluation with ablation analysis.} Rigorous evaluation across 250 questions with an ablation study quantifying each retrieval strategy's contribution, and practical guidelines for when GraphRAG outperforms VectorRAG.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work. Section~\ref{sec:architecture} presents the system architecture. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the experimental methodology. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{From VectorRAG to GraphRAG}

RAG was introduced by \citet{lewis2020rag} to ground LLM responses in retrieved documents. In its standard form (VectorRAG), documents are chunked, embedded, and stored in a vector database; at query time, the most semantically similar chunks are retrieved and provided as context. While effective for single-hop factual queries, VectorRAG struggles with multi-hop reasoning, structured relationships, and source attribution.

GraphRAG extends this paradigm by integrating knowledge graphs. \citet{edge2024graphrag} proposed using LLMs to derive knowledge graphs from documents and pre-generate community summaries via the Leiden algorithm \citep{traag2019leiden}. Two comprehensive surveys formalize the GraphRAG workflow: \citet{peng2024graphrag} define a taxonomy spanning graph-based indexing, retrieval, and generation, while \citet{han2025graphragsurvey} propose a holistic framework. Architectural variants include LightRAG \citep{guo2024lightrag} with dual-level retrieval, HippoRAG \citep{gutierrez2024hipporag} with neurobiological memory models, and SubgraphRAG \citep{li2025subgraphrag} demonstrating competitive results with smaller LLMs.

Critical evaluations have tempered initial optimism. \citet{unbiased2025} find that GraphRAG win rates are generally lower than reported under unbiased evaluation, while \citet{whenusegraphs2025} show that GraphRAG frequently underperforms VectorRAG in many tasks but excels specifically in multi-hop reasoning. These findings motivate our rigorous comparative evaluation.

\subsection{RAG for financial applications}

HybridRAG \citep{sarmah2024hybridrag} (BlackRock/NVIDIA) combines knowledge graph and vector retrieval for earnings call transcripts but lacks community detection and token optimization. FinReflectKG \citep{arun2025finreflectkg} constructs financial knowledge graphs from SEC 10-K filings with 64.8\% quality compliance. GraphCompliance \citep{graphcompliance2025} achieves 4.1--7.2 pp improvement in micro-F1 for GDPR compliance. FinanceBench \citep{islam2023financebench} provides a standardized evaluation framework for financial QA from SEC filings. These works address subsets of our requirements but none combines domain ontology, multi-strategy retrieval, and token optimization.

\subsection{Token efficiency}

Token consumption is critical for enterprise deployments. TERAG \citep{terag2025} achieves 80\% of GraphRAG accuracy with 3--11\% of output tokens. LazyGraphRAG \citep{lazygraphrag2025} reduces indexation costs to 0.1\% of full GraphRAG. PolyG \citep{polyg2025} achieves 95\% token reduction via dynamic Cypher query planning. \citet{benchmarkretrieval2025} find that GraphRAG delivers 1.5$\times$ better accuracy but with 2.4$\times$ higher latency, motivating our parallel retrieval approach.

\section{System architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

This section presents the architecture of the proposed multi-strategy GraphRAG system. We describe the formal notation and ontology (Section~\ref{sec:notation}), the ingestion pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:ingestion}), and the multi-strategy retrieval engine (Section~\ref{sec:retrieval}). Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} provides an overview.

\subsection{Formal notation and ontology}
\label{sec:notation}

\textbf{Knowledge graph.} We define $G = (V, E, \varphi, \psi)$ where $V$ is the set of entities, $E \subseteq V \times V$ is the set of relationships, $\varphi: V \rightarrow \mathcal{T}_V$ maps entities to types, and $\psi: E \rightarrow \mathcal{T}_E$ maps edges to relationship types.

\textbf{Entity types.} The financial domain ontology defines 10 entity types:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\mathcal{T}_V = \{&\textsc{Regulation}, \textsc{Article}, \textsc{Requirement}, \textsc{RiskCategory}, \\
&\textsc{FinancialMetric}, \textsc{Company}, \textsc{Subsidiary}, \\
&\textsc{Jurisdiction}, \textsc{Control}, \textsc{AuditFinding}\}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Relationship types.} The ontology defines 14 relationship types:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\mathcal{T}_E = \{&\textsc{Regulates}, \textsc{Requires}, \textsc{Defines}, \textsc{ExposesTo}, \textsc{SubsidiaryOf}, \\
&\textsc{OperatesIn}, \textsc{AuditedBy}, \textsc{Mitigates}, \textsc{References}, \\
&\textsc{Supersedes}, \textsc{ComposedOf}, \textsc{ReportsTo}, \textsc{ValidatedBy}, \textsc{CompliesWith}\}
\end{split}
\end{equation}

A triple $t = (s, p, o) \in V \times \mathcal{T}_E \times V$ represents a relationship with confidence $c(t) \in [0, 1]$. The document corpus is segmented into chunks $\mathcal{C} = \{c_1, \ldots, c_K\}$ with embeddings $\mathbf{e}(c_k) \in \mathbb{R}^{384}$ (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) and content hashes for incremental processing. Communities $\Gamma = \{\gamma_1, \ldots, \gamma_L\}$ are computed via the Leiden algorithm with LLM-generated summaries.

Each query $q$ has an intent $\iota(q) \in \mathcal{I}$ where $\mathcal{I} = \{\textsc{Validation},$ $\textsc{Comparison},$ $\textsc{Recommendation},$ $\textsc{Explanation},$ $\textsc{CodeExample},$ $\textsc{General}\}$.

\subsection{Ingestion pipeline}
\label{sec:ingestion}

The ingestion pipeline transforms raw financial documents into a knowledge graph through five stages, described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:ingestion}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Ingestion Pipeline}
\label{alg:ingestion}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Document corpus $\mathcal{D}$, ontology $(\mathcal{T}_V, \mathcal{T}_E)$, alias dictionary $\mathcal{A}$, fuzzy threshold $\theta = 0.85$
\Ensure Knowledge graph $G = (V, E, \varphi, \psi)$, communities $\Gamma$
\State $\mathcal{C} \gets \emptyset$
\For{each document $d \in \mathcal{D}$}
    \If{$\text{SHA-256}(\text{content}(d)) \neq h_{\text{cache}}(d)$}
        \State $\mathcal{C} \gets \mathcal{C} \cup \text{SelectParser}(\text{type}(d)).\text{chunk}(d)$
    \Else
        \State $\mathcal{C} \gets \mathcal{C} \cup \text{LoadFromCache}(d)$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\For{each chunk $c_k \in \mathcal{C}$} \Comment{Via Batch API for 50\% savings}
    \State $\mathcal{T}_{\text{raw}} \gets \mathcal{T}_{\text{raw}} \cup \text{LLM-Extract}(c_k, \mathcal{T}_V, \mathcal{T}_E)$
\EndFor
\For{each triple $(s, p, o) \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{raw}}$}
    \State $s' \gets \text{Resolve}(s, \mathcal{A}, \theta)$ \Comment{Exact $\to$ case-insensitive $\to$ fuzzy}
    \State $o' \gets \text{Resolve}(o, \mathcal{A}, \theta)$
    \State Validate against ontology; discard invalid types
\EndFor
\State $G \gets \text{LoadGraph}(\mathcal{T}_{\text{resolved}})$ \Comment{Cypher MERGE}
\State $\Gamma \gets \text{Leiden}(G, r=1.0)$; generate summaries and embeddings
\State \Return $G, \Gamma$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Chunking} dispatches documents to specialized parsers: AST-based for Python (preserving class/function boundaries), header-based for Markdown (preserving regulatory document hierarchy), and cell-grouping for notebooks. Content hashes enable incremental processing.

\textbf{Triple extraction} processes each chunk via the LLM API with an ontology-constrained prompt. For batches of 50+ chunks, Batch API processing provides a 50\% cost discount.

\textbf{Entity resolution} operates at three levels: (1)~exact match against an alias dictionary ($\sim$130 aliases), (2)~case-insensitive normalization, and (3)~fuzzy matching via sequence similarity ($\theta = 0.85$). Duplicate triples retain the highest-confidence instance.

\textbf{Graph loading} uses Cypher MERGE operations with 10 uniqueness constraints and full-text indices.

\textbf{Community detection} uses the Leiden algorithm \citep{traag2019leiden}, which guarantees well-connected communities---a property critical for financial graphs where regulatory entities must be correctly grouped. Each community receives an LLM-generated summary with a corresponding embedding.


\subsection{Multi-strategy retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

The central contribution is the multi-strategy retrieval engine that executes three strategies in parallel and fuses results using intent-aware weighting. Algorithm~\ref{alg:retrieval} provides the pseudocode.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Multi-Strategy Intent-Aware Retrieval}
\label{alg:retrieval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Query $q$, knowledge graph $G$, communities $\Gamma$, weight table $\mathbf{W}$, token budget $B$
\Ensure Ranked context $\mathcal{R}$, intent $\iota$
\State $\iota \gets \text{ClassifyIntent}(q)$ \Comment{Pattern matching for 6 intent types}
\State $\mathcal{E}_q \gets \text{ExtractEntities}(q, \mathcal{A})$ \Comment{Longest-match-first}
\State $(w_g, w_v, w_c) \gets \mathbf{W}[\iota]$ \Comment{Intent-specific weights}
\State $\mathcal{R}_g \gets \text{GraphRetriever}(G, \mathcal{E}_q, \iota)$ \textbf{in parallel}
\State $\mathcal{R}_v \gets \text{VectorRetriever}(q, G, \iota)$ \textbf{in parallel}
\State $\mathcal{R}_c \gets \text{CommunityRetriever}(q, \mathcal{E}_q, \Gamma)$ \textbf{in parallel}
\For{each result $r \in \mathcal{R}_g \cup \mathcal{R}_v \cup \mathcal{R}_c$}
    \State $\text{score}(r) \gets w_g \cdot \hat{s}_g(r) + w_v \cdot \hat{s}_v(r) + w_c \cdot \hat{s}_c(r)$
\EndFor
\State $\mathcal{R} \gets \text{Deduplicate, Sort, TruncateByBudget}(\mathcal{R}, B, \text{min}=3)$
\State \Return $\mathcal{R}, \iota$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Intent parser}

The intent parser classifies each query into one of six types using pattern matching against bilingual keyword dictionaries. Table~\ref{tab:weights} presents the weight configuration.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Intent-aware retrieval weights for each query type.}
\label{tab:weights}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}llccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Intent} & \textbf{Typical patterns} & $w_g$ & $w_v$ & $w_c$ \\
\midrule
\textsc{Validation} & validate, check, verify & 0.7 & 0.2 & 0.1 \\
\textsc{Comparison} & compare, versus, between & 0.5 & 0.3 & 0.2 \\
\textsc{Recommendation} & best for, which model & 0.3 & 0.4 & 0.3 \\

\textsc{General} & (default) & 0.3 & 0.3 & 0.4 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Validation queries benefit most from graph traversal because they require verifying specific entity relationships. Code example queries favor vector retrieval for semantic similarity to code snippets. General queries favor community retrieval to leverage high-level topic summaries.

\subsubsection{Graph retriever}

The graph retriever performs N-hop traversal from identified entities using Cypher queries adapted to the detected intent. For each entity $e \in \mathcal{E}_q$, a depth-limited traversal ($\text{max\_depth} = 2$) computes a distance-decay score:
\begin{equation}
\text{score}_g(v) = 1.0 - \frac{\text{hop\_distance}(v)}{2 \cdot \text{max\_depth}}
\label{eq:graph-score}
\end{equation}
yielding scores in $[0.5, 1.0]$. Intent-specific patterns prioritize different relationship types (e.g., validation queries follow \textsc{Validates} and \textsc{CompliesWith} edges).

\subsubsection{Vector retriever}

The vector retriever computes the query embedding using sentence-transformers (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, $d=384$) and searches the Neo4j vector index for chunks with cosine similarity above 0.5, filtered by chunk type based on intent. Each retrieved chunk is enriched with its graph neighborhood.

\subsubsection{Community retriever}

The community retriever uses a dual approach: (1)~entity membership lookup (score 1.0 for direct matches) and (2)~cosine similarity between query and community summary embeddings. This ensures both direct entity matches and topically relevant communities are retrieved.

\subsubsection{Fusion and ranking}

Results are fused using:
\begin{equation}
\text{score}_{\text{final}}(r) = \sum_{s \in \{g, v, c\}} w_s(\iota) \cdot \hat{s}_s(r)
\label{eq:fusion}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{s}_s(r) \in [0, 1]$ is the min-max normalized score. Results are deduplicated, ranked, and truncated to the token budget $B$ (default: 4,000 tokens) with a minimum of 3 results.

\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Metrics}

We evaluate four dimensions: (1)~\textbf{Hallucination reduction}: faithfulness (proportion of claims grounded in context), factual accuracy (expert-assessed), and grounding rate (proportion of responses with source attribution). (2)~\textbf{Token economy}: context tokens per query, total tokens, and cost per 1,000 queries. (3)~\textbf{Processing speed}: end-to-end latency (p50/p95) and cache hit rate. (4)~\textbf{Benchmark performance}: weighted score combining entity coverage (40\%), concept coverage (30\%), source attribution (20\%), and response quality (10\%).

\subsection{Datasets}

\textbf{Regulatory question set (200 questions).} Manually curated questions covering Basel~III and LGPD, with expert-annotated ground truth. Categories: regulatory compliance (80), financial analysis (50), multi-hop reasoning (40), comparison (20), and audit (10).

\textbf{Financial benchmark (50 questions).} Domain-specific benchmark following the FinanceBench methodology \citep{islam2023financebench}: regulatory compliance (15), financial analysis (12), multi-hop reasoning (10), comparison (8), and audit (5).

\textbf{Document corpus.} 150 financial documents comprising regulatory frameworks, SEC 10-K filings from S\&P 100 companies, central bank resolutions, and 50 simulated audit reports.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}

Hallucination is assessed via an LLM-as-judge framework, validated by two domain experts on a 10\% sample (20 questions) with inter-rater agreement via Cohen's kappa. Token counts are measured at each pipeline stage. Latency measurements use medians of 3 runs. All experiments use seed 42 for reproducibility on a server with 8 CPU cores, 32~GB RAM, Neo4j 5.x, and PostgreSQL 16.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key findings}

Five key findings emerge from the evaluation:

\textbf{Finding 1: Multi-strategy retrieval with intent-aware fusion consistently outperforms single-strategy approaches.} The 14.47 pp faithfulness improvement over VectorRAG and the ablation study confirming all three strategies are complementary validate that different query types benefit from different retrieval strategies.

\textbf{Finding 2: Structured graph retrieval reduces token consumption while improving quality.} The 70\% token reduction versus full-document retrieval is achieved not through compression but by retrieving only the relevant subgraph, resulting in higher-quality context.

\textbf{Finding 3: Parallel retrieval eliminates the latency penalty.} With parallel execution and caching, the system is 33\% faster than VectorRAG despite executing three retrieval strategies.

\textbf{Finding 4: The advantage is most pronounced for structured, relational queries.} The audit category shows a 40.18 pp advantage over VectorRAG, while financial analysis shows 24.98 pp---confirming that graph-based retrieval is most valuable when queries traverse entity relationships.

\textbf{Finding 5: Community retrieval provides unique value for exploratory queries.} Despite contributing only 4.74 pp overall, community retrieval is essential for general queries requiring broad topical understanding.


\subsection{When to use GraphRAG versus VectorRAG}
\label{sec:disc-when}

Based on our results and consistent with \citet{whenusegraphs2025}, we offer practical guidelines:

\textbf{GraphRAG is recommended when:} (1)~multi-hop reasoning is required, where our evaluation shows a 12.84 pp faithfulness advantage; (2)~source attribution is critical, with a 41.34 pp grounding rate advantage; (3)~the domain has stable entity-relationship structure; and (4)~query volume justifies the indexation investment (break-even at $\sim$687 queries).

\textbf{VectorRAG may suffice when:} (1)~queries are predominantly single-hop lookups; (2)~the corpus changes frequently and knowledge graph maintenance is costly; (3)~source attribution is not mandated; and (4)~strict latency requirements exist without caching feasibility.


\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc-limitations}

\textbf{L1: Knowledge graph quality depends on LLM extraction.} The knowledge graph is constructed through LLM-based triple extraction, which may introduce errors. A formal evaluation of extraction quality (precision, recall) was not conducted.

\textbf{L2: Simulated data.} The audit application uses simulated reports rather than real institutional data.

\textbf{L3: API cost variability.} Token costs reflect pricing at evaluation time. Relative cost comparisons between systems are more stable than absolute costs.

\textbf{L4: Domain-specific results.} The evaluation uses financial regulatory documents. Absolute performance may not generalize to other domains, though the architecture is domain-agnostic via its configurable ontology.

\textbf{L5: Single LLM provider.} All experiments use a single LLM provider. Performance may vary across providers.

\textbf{L6: Evaluation dataset size.} The 250-question evaluation is sufficient for demonstrating capabilities but larger-scale evaluation would strengthen statistical confidence.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a multi-strategy GraphRAG architecture and compared it systematically against VectorRAG for financial document processing. The system integrates a domain-specific financial ontology with an intent-aware retrieval engine that fuses graph traversal, vector similarity, and community summarization in parallel.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates that: (1)~multi-strategy GraphRAG achieves 87.35\% faithfulness versus 72.88\% for VectorRAG, with 96.38\% source attribution; (2)~structured graph retrieval reduces token consumption by 70\% compared to full-document retrieval; (3)~parallel execution with caching achieves 797ms median latency, 33\% faster than VectorRAG; and (4)~the ablation study confirms all three retrieval strategies are complementary, with graph retrieval contributing 16.13 pp, vector retrieval 9.51 pp, and community retrieval 4.74 pp.

The practical guidelines for when to adopt GraphRAG versus VectorRAG provide actionable recommendations for practitioners. GraphRAG is most valuable when queries require multi-hop reasoning, source attribution is legally mandated, and the domain has stable entity-relationship structure. VectorRAG remains appropriate for single-hop lookups in frequently changing corpora without strict attribution requirements.

\textbf{Future work.} Three directions are identified: (1)~evaluation with real institutional data to strengthen external validity; (2)~temporal knowledge graphs to reason about regulatory changes over time \citep{li2024findkg}; and (3)~cost optimization with smaller specialized models for extraction and classification \citep{li2025subgraphrag}.
