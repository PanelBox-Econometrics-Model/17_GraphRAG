## Overview and Purpose of Pillar 2

Pillar 2 of the Basel III framework establishes the Supervisory Review Process (SRP), which serves as a critical complement to the minimum capital requirements defined under Pillar 1 and the market discipline mechanisms of Pillar 3. The primary objective of Pillar 2 is to ensure that banks maintain adequate capital to support all material risks in their business, going beyond the regulatory minimums prescribed by Pillar 1. It also encourages banks to develop and employ improved risk management techniques for monitoring and managing their exposures.

The Supervisory Review Process was originally introduced in the Basel II framework and has been substantially reinforced under Basel III following the lessons of the 2007-2009 global financial crisis. The BCBS recognized that minimum capital requirements alone are insufficient to ensure the safety and soundness of individual institutions and the stability of the broader financial system. Pillar 2 addresses this gap by requiring a forward-looking assessment of risks that may not be fully captured by Pillar 1 calculations, including concentration risk, interest rate risk in the banking book, and strategic risk.

Pillar 2 operates through a dialogue between banks and their supervisors. Banks are expected to conduct their own internal assessments of capital adequacy relative to their risk profiles, while supervisors are responsible for evaluating those assessments and taking appropriate action when deficiencies are identified. This dual responsibility creates a framework of shared accountability that strengthens the overall resilience of the banking sector.

The BCBS guidance on Pillar 2, as outlined in the document "Supervisory Review Process" (SRP), establishes that supervisory authorities should intervene at an early stage to prevent capital from falling below minimum levels. Supervisors retain the authority to impose additional capital buffers and to require remedial measures when banks are found to operate with inadequate capital or risk management practices.

## Four Key Principles of Supervisory Review

The Supervisory Review Process is built upon four foundational principles articulated by the BCBS. These principles define the respective responsibilities of banks and supervisors and form the backbone of the Pillar 2 framework.

**Principle 1** states that banks should have a process for assessing their overall capital adequacy in relation to their risk profile and a strategy for maintaining their capital levels. This principle establishes the requirement for the Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP), through which banks must identify, measure, and aggregate all material risks and determine the capital needed to cover those risks under both normal and stressed conditions.

**Principle 2** requires that supervisors should review and evaluate banks' internal capital adequacy assessments and strategies, as well as their ability to monitor and ensure compliance with regulatory capital ratios. Where supervisors are not satisfied with the results of this process, they should take appropriate supervisory action. This includes the Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP).

**Principle 3** establishes that supervisors should expect banks to operate above the minimum regulatory capital ratios and should have the ability to require banks to hold capital in excess of the Pillar 1 minimum. This principle recognizes that Pillar 1 requirements represent a floor, not a ceiling, and that banks with higher risk profiles or weaker risk management should maintain commensurately higher capital levels.

**Principle 4** directs that supervisors should seek to intervene at an early stage to prevent capital from falling below the minimum levels required to support the risk characteristics of a particular bank. Rapid remedial action should be taken if capital is not maintained or restored. Supervisory responses may include intensified monitoring, requiring a capital restoration plan, restricting dividends, or requiring the bank to raise additional capital immediately.

## Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP)

The Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP) is the cornerstone of Pillar 2 from the perspective of the banking institution. Under BCBS guidelines, every bank is required to have an ICAAP that is proportionate to the nature, scale, and complexity of its activities. The ICAAP must be a comprehensive, institution-wide process that considers all material risks to which the bank is exposed.

A robust ICAAP encompasses several key elements: identification and measurement of all material risks, including those not covered or not fully captured under Pillar 1; setting of internal capital targets that are consistent with the bank's risk profile, operating environment, and strategic plans; and integration of capital planning with business planning and risk appetite frameworks. The ICAAP must also incorporate forward-looking elements, including stress testing and scenario analysis.

Board oversight and senior management involvement are essential components of the ICAAP. The bank's board of directors bears ultimate responsibility for ensuring that the institution has adequate capital relative to its risks. Senior management must develop the processes for assessing capital adequacy, set appropriate capital targets, and ensure that capital planning is integrated into the institution's overall strategic planning process.

The ICAAP should produce a documented assessment that is reviewed and approved by the board at least annually. This documentation must include a description of the bank's risk appetite, the methodologies used for risk identification and quantification, the results of stress testing, and the capital plan. The ICAAP is subject to supervisory review as part of the SREP, and banks must be prepared to demonstrate the soundness and reliability of their internal assessments to their supervisors.

## Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP)

The Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP) represents the supervisory side of the Pillar 2 framework. Through the SREP, supervisory authorities conduct a comprehensive evaluation of individual banks' risk profiles, risk governance, capital adequacy, and liquidity positions. The SREP is designed to ensure that banks have adequate arrangements, strategies, processes, and mechanisms to maintain sufficient capital and liquidity to cover their risks.

Supervisors assess the quality and comprehensiveness of the bank's ICAAP, including the appropriateness of the methodologies employed, the accuracy of risk quantification, and the adequacy of internal capital targets. The SREP evaluation also considers qualitative factors such as the effectiveness of the bank's governance framework, the competence of senior management, and the quality of internal controls and audit functions.

Based on the SREP assessment, supervisors may require banks to hold additional capital above Pillar 1 minimums, known as Pillar 2 capital requirements (P2R) or Pillar 2 capital guidance (P2G). Supervisors may also impose qualitative requirements, such as improvements to risk management processes, governance structures, or data quality. The SREP outcome is communicated to the bank and typically includes a timeline for addressing identified deficiencies.

The BCBS expects that the SREP is conducted on a regular basis, with the frequency and intensity proportionate to the systemic importance, size, and risk profile of the institution. Globally systemically important banks (G-SIBs) are typically subject to more frequent and intensive supervisory review than smaller institutions.

## Interest Rate Risk in the Banking Book (IRRBB)

Interest Rate Risk in the Banking Book (IRRBB) is one of the most significant risks addressed under Pillar 2. The BCBS issued updated standards on IRRBB in April 2016 (BCBS Standard d368), which require banks to measure and control their exposure to changes in interest rates that affect the economic value and earnings of their banking book positions.

IRRBB arises from mismatches in the repricing characteristics of a bank's assets and liabilities. The three main sub-types of IRRBB are gap risk (arising from the timing of instrument rate changes), basis risk (arising from imperfect correlation in the adjustment of rates on different instruments with otherwise similar repricing characteristics), and option risk (arising from options embedded in banking book instruments, such as prepayment options on loans).

Banks are required to measure IRRBB using both economic value of equity (EVE) and net interest income (NII) approaches under a range of prescribed interest rate shock scenarios. The BCBS has defined six standardized shock scenarios for EVE and two for NII. Banks whose IRRBB exposure exceeds supervisory thresholds, defined as a decline in EVE greater than 15 percent of Tier 1 capital, are identified as outlier banks and may be subject to additional supervisory scrutiny and potential capital add-ons.

Effective IRRBB management requires robust governance frameworks, clearly defined risk appetite limits, comprehensive measurement systems, and regular reporting to senior management and the board. Banks must also incorporate IRRBB into their ICAAP and stress testing programs.

## Credit Concentration Risk

Credit concentration risk refers to the risk arising from an uneven distribution of exposures to individual counterparties, groups of connected counterparties, industries, economic sectors, or geographic regions. The BCBS has identified credit concentration risk as a material risk that is not fully captured by Pillar 1 capital requirements, making it a primary focus of Pillar 2 assessment.

There are two main dimensions of concentration risk: single-name concentration, which arises from large exposures to individual counterparties or groups of connected counterparties; and sectoral concentration, which arises from imperfect diversification across industries, geographies, or asset classes. Both dimensions can amplify losses during economic downturns and contribute to systemic risk when concentrations are correlated across institutions.

Under Pillar 2, banks are expected to have policies and processes for identifying, measuring, monitoring, and controlling concentration risk. This includes setting internal limits on large exposures, conducting regular portfolio concentration analyses, and incorporating concentration risk into the ICAAP. Supervisors evaluate the adequacy of these measures as part of the SREP and may impose additional capital requirements where concentrations are deemed excessive relative to the bank's capital base and risk management capacity.

## Liquidity Risk Management

Although the Basel III framework introduced dedicated quantitative liquidity standards under Pillar 1, including the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) and the Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR), Pillar 2 retains an important role in the supervisory assessment of liquidity risk management. The BCBS "Principles for Sound Liquidity Risk Management and Supervision" (BCBS144, September 2008) establishes comprehensive guidance that supervisors evaluate under the SREP.

Banks are expected to maintain a robust liquidity risk management framework that includes clearly articulated liquidity risk tolerance, appropriate governance and oversight structures, comprehensive measurement and monitoring systems, and well-developed contingency funding plans. The framework should cover all material sources of liquidity risk, including intraday liquidity risk, contingent liquidity risk from off-balance-sheet commitments, and cross-currency and cross-border liquidity risk.

Supervisors assess whether banks' liquidity risk management practices are commensurate with their size, complexity, and risk profile. Where deficiencies are identified, supervisors may require banks to enhance their liquidity buffers, improve their funding profiles, or strengthen their contingency planning. The integration of liquidity risk assessment into the broader SREP framework ensures that capital adequacy and liquidity adequacy are considered holistically.

## Stress Testing Requirements

Stress testing is a fundamental component of the Pillar 2 framework and a critical tool within the ICAAP. The BCBS guidance on stress testing, including the "Stress Testing Principles" issued in October 2018 (BCBS239 update), establishes expectations for banks and supervisors regarding the design, implementation, and use of stress tests.

Banks are required to conduct regular stress tests that assess the impact of severe but plausible adverse scenarios on their capital adequacy, profitability, and liquidity. Stress testing programs should cover all material risk types, including credit risk, market risk, operational risk, interest rate risk, and liquidity risk. Scenarios should be forward-looking and should incorporate both institution-specific and system-wide stress events.

The results of stress testing should directly inform capital planning and risk management decisions. Banks should integrate stress testing outcomes into the ICAAP, ensuring that internal capital targets are sufficient to absorb losses under stressed conditions without breaching minimum regulatory requirements. Senior management and the board should regularly review stress test results and take action to address identified vulnerabilities.

Supervisors use bank-level and system-wide stress testing as an input into the SREP. Supervisory stress tests may be conducted independently of banks' internal exercises and can serve as a basis for imposing additional capital requirements or buffers under Pillar 2.

## Corporate Governance and Risk Management

Pillar 2 places significant emphasis on the quality of corporate governance and risk management as determinants of a bank's overall safety and soundness. The BCBS "Corporate Governance Principles for Banks" (revised in July 2015) and the "Principles for the Sound Management of Operational Risk" provide detailed expectations that supervisors evaluate under the SREP.

Banks are expected to have governance structures that provide effective oversight of risk-taking activities. This includes a board of directors with appropriate expertise and independence, a clear organizational structure with well-defined lines of responsibility, effective internal control and audit functions, and a risk management function that is independent from business lines. The Chief Risk Officer (CRO) should have sufficient authority, stature, and resources to fulfill the risk management mandate.

The risk management framework should encompass a comprehensive risk appetite statement approved by the board, policies and procedures for all material risk types, robust risk measurement and reporting systems, and effective escalation mechanisms for limit breaches and emerging risks. The framework must be subject to regular independent review to ensure its continued effectiveness and relevance.

Supervisors assess governance and risk management quality as part of the SREP and may impose qualitative requirements, such as changes to governance structures, enhancements to risk management processes, or restrictions on certain business activities, where deficiencies are identified. Poor governance and weak risk management may also serve as a basis for requiring higher Pillar 2 capital buffers.

## Pillar 2 Capital Add-Ons

Pillar 2 capital add-ons represent additional capital that supervisors may require banks to hold above the Pillar 1 minimum. These add-ons are determined through the SREP process and are tailored to each institution's specific risk profile and circumstances. The BCBS framework distinguishes between Pillar 2 Requirements (P2R) and Pillar 2 Guidance (P2G).

Pillar 2 Requirements (P2R) are binding capital requirements that address risks not covered or not fully captured under Pillar 1. Common drivers of P2R include credit concentration risk, IRRBB, settlement risk, securitization risk not captured in Pillar 1, and model risk arising from the use of internal models. P2R are legally binding and a breach triggers mandatory supervisory intervention.

Pillar 2 Guidance (P2G) represents a supervisory expectation of the capital level that a bank should maintain above its binding capital requirements. P2G is typically informed by the results of supervisory stress tests and is intended to ensure that banks can absorb losses under adverse conditions without breaching their combined buffer requirement or P2R. While P2G is not legally binding in the same manner as P2R, a failure to meet P2G triggers a supervisory dialogue and may lead to more intensive monitoring.

The calibration of Pillar 2 capital add-ons requires supervisory judgment and must be based on a transparent methodology. Supervisors should communicate the rationale for add-ons clearly to the bank and provide an opportunity for the institution to present its own views before final determinations are made.

## Supervisory Intervention and Corrective Actions

The Pillar 2 framework provides supervisors with a range of tools for intervention when banks are found to have inadequate capital, deficient risk management, or other material weaknesses. The principle of early intervention is central to the framework, reflecting the BCBS view that prompt action reduces the likelihood of bank failures and minimizes their cost to the financial system and to depositors.

Supervisory responses are typically graduated and proportionate to the severity of the identified deficiencies. Initial measures may include enhanced monitoring, increased reporting requirements, or the issuance of supervisory recommendations for improvement. Where deficiencies are more significant, supervisors may impose formal requirements, such as mandatory capital restoration plans, restrictions on dividend payments and share buybacks, limitations on business expansion, or requirements to divest particular risk exposures.

In cases of severe deterioration, supervisors have the authority to require immediate capital increases, to impose restrictions on management activities, or to initiate resolution proceedings. The BCBS framework emphasizes that supervisors should have clear legal authority to take these actions and should be willing to exercise that authority in a timely manner.

Cross-border supervisory cooperation is a critical aspect of Pillar 2 implementation for internationally active banks. Home and host supervisors should maintain effective channels of communication, share relevant information on the risk profiles of banking groups, and coordinate their supervisory responses to ensure consistency and avoid gaps in oversight. Supervisory colleges, as promoted by the BCBS and the Financial Stability Board (FSB), serve as the primary mechanism for facilitating this coordination.
