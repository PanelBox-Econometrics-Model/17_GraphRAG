# LGPD - Brazilian General Data Protection Law (Lei 13.709/2018)

The Brazilian General Data Protection Law (LGPD), enacted on August 14, 2018, establishes comprehensive rules for the collection, processing, storage, and sharing of personal data in Brazil. It applies to any natural person or legal entity that processes personal data within Brazilian territory or targeting individuals located in Brazil. The LGPD is enforced by the National Data Protection Authority (ANPD).

---

## Chapter I - General Provisions (Articles 1-6)

### Article 1 - Scope

This law governs the processing of personal data, including by digital means, by any natural person or legal entity, whether public or private, with the purpose of protecting the fundamental rights of freedom, privacy, and the free development of the personality of natural persons. It applies to all data processing carried out in Brazilian territory regardless of the processor's headquarters or the location of the data.

### Article 2 - Foundations

Data protection is grounded in respect for privacy, informational self-determination, freedom of expression, inviolability of intimacy, economic and technological development, free enterprise, consumer protection, and human rights and dignity.

### Article 3 - Territorial Application

The LGPD applies to any processing operation carried out in Brazil, when the activity targets data subjects in Brazil, or when personal data was collected in Brazilian territory. The law applies regardless of the controller's or processor's country of headquarters.

### Article 4 - Exclusions

The LGPD does not apply to processing by natural persons for exclusively private non-economic purposes, nor to processing exclusively for journalistic, artistic, academic, public safety, national defense, state security, or criminal investigation purposes.

### Article 5 - Definitions

This article establishes key definitions used throughout the law. "Personal data" means any information related to an identified or identifiable natural person. "Sensitive personal data" includes data on racial or ethnic origin, religious conviction, political opinion, union membership, health, sex life, genetic data, or biometric data. "Controller" is the person or entity responsible for decisions regarding data processing. "Processor" is the person or entity that processes data on behalf of the controller. Other defined terms include "data protection officer," "anonymization," "consent," "blocking," "database," and "international data transfer."

### Article 6 - Principles

Processing must observe the principles of: purpose, adequacy, necessity, free access, data quality, transparency, security, prevention, non-discrimination, and accountability. Controllers must demonstrate compliance with these principles.

## Chapter II - Personal Data Processing (Articles 7-16)

### Section I - Requirements for Processing (Articles 7-10)

### Article 7 - Legal Bases for Processing

Personal data may only be processed when at least one of ten legal bases applies: the data subject's consent; compliance with a legal or regulatory obligation by the controller; execution of public policies by the public administration; research by study entities using anonymization when possible; execution of a contract at the data subject's request; exercise of rights in judicial, administrative, or arbitration proceedings; protection of life or physical safety; health protection by health professionals or entities; the controller's legitimate interests; or credit protection.

### Article 8 - Consent Requirements

Consent must be provided in writing or other demonstrable means, as a separate clause distinct from other contractual matters. The controller bears the burden of proving valid consent, and consent may be revoked at any time via a free and facilitated procedure.

### Article 9 - Right to Information

Data subjects have the right to information about the purpose, duration, controller identity, data sharing practices, and agent responsibilities related to processing. Consent based on misleading, ambiguous, or abusive information is void.

### Article 10 - Legitimate Interest

Legitimate interest as a legal basis requires concrete, legitimate purposes such as supporting the controller's activities or protecting the data subject. Only strictly necessary data may be processed, and the controller must adopt transparency measures.

### Section II - Processing of Sensitive Personal Data (Articles 11-13)

### Article 11 - Conditions for Processing Sensitive Data

Sensitive data processing requires specific and highlighted consent, or may proceed without consent when indispensable for legal obligation compliance, public policy, research with anonymization, exercise of rights, protection of life, health care by professionals, or fraud prevention.

### Article 12 - Anonymized Data

Anonymized data is not considered personal data unless the anonymization can be reversed using reasonable technical means. Data used for behavioral profiling of an identifiable person may still be treated as personal data.

### Article 13 - Research and Public Health

Health research data must be processed exclusively within research bodies in controlled, secure environments, with anonymization or pseudonymization whenever possible. Results must never reveal individual personal data.

### Section III - Processing of Children's and Adolescents' Data (Article 14)

### Article 14 - Children's and Adolescents' Data

The processing of personal data of children and adolescents must be carried out in their best interest. Processing of children's data requires specific and highlighted consent from at least one parent or legal guardian. Controllers must make reasonable efforts to verify that consent was provided by the parent or guardian using available technologies. Controllers must not condition children's participation in games, internet applications, or other activities on the provision of personal data beyond what is strictly necessary.

### Section IV - Completion of Processing (Articles 15-16)

### Article 15 - Termination of Processing

Processing ends when the purpose is achieved, the period expires, the data subject revokes consent, or the ANPD determines termination due to a violation.

### Article 16 - Deletion of Data

Data must be deleted after processing ends, except for legal obligations, research with anonymization, lawful transfer to third parties, or exclusive controller use with anonymization.

## Chapter III - Rights of the Data Subject (Articles 17-22)

### Article 17 - Ownership of Personal Data

Every natural person is guaranteed ownership of their personal data and the fundamental rights of freedom, intimacy, and privacy.

### Article 18 - Data Subject Rights

The data subject has the right to obtain from the controller at any time upon request: confirmation of the existence of processing; access to the data; correction of incomplete, inaccurate, or outdated data; anonymization, blocking, or deletion of unnecessary or excessive data; portability of data to another service or product provider; deletion of personal data processed with consent; information about public and private entities with which the controller has shared data; information about the possibility of denying consent and its consequences; and revocation of consent.

### Article 19 - Confirmation and Access

Confirmation and data access must be provided within 15 days of the request, in clear format or detailed statement. Data subjects may request a complete electronic copy of their data when processing is based on consent or contract.

### Article 20 - Automated Decision Review

Data subjects may request review of decisions made solely through automated processing that affect their interests, including profiling decisions. Controllers must disclose criteria and procedures used, subject to trade secrets.

### Article 21 - Defense of Interests

Personal data related to the exercise of a data subject's rights may not be used against them. Data use must not cause harm through discriminatory or abusive practices.

### Article 22 - Protection in Judicial Proceedings

Data subject interests may be defended individually or collectively in court. Data subjects may also petition the ANPD for protection of their rights.

---

## Chapter IV - Processing of Personal Data by the Government (Articles 23-30)

### Article 23 - Public Administration Processing

Public entities must process personal data for the performance of legal duties and public policy execution, with clear and updated information provided to data subjects about the legal basis, purpose, and procedures.

### Article 24 - State-Owned Enterprises

State-owned enterprises operating in competitive markets follow private-entity rules. When implementing public policies, they follow public-entity rules.

### Article 25 - Data in Interoperable Format

Public data must be maintained in interoperable, structured formats for shared use in public policy execution and service provision, observing security and protection requirements.

### Article 26 - Shared Use by Government

The shared use of personal data by the government must meet specific public purposes in compliance with the law. Shared use for purposes incompatible with the original collection purpose is prohibited. Data sharing between government entities must be communicated to the ANPD. Transfer of personal data to private entities is forbidden except for decentralized execution of public activities, publicly accessible data, or specific legal provisions.

### Article 27 - External Communication of Government Data

Sharing personal data held by public entities with private entities requires data subject consent, except for fraud prevention or when data is publicly accessible.

### Article 28 - Notary and Registry Bodies

Notary and registry bodies acting under public delegation must comply with this law and ensure data transparency and protection per ANPD standards.

### Article 29 - ANPD Oversight of Government

The ANPD may request information from public entities at any time, issue opinions and recommendations, and suggest adoption of standards and good practices for government data processing.

### Article 30 - ANPD Report on Government Processing

The ANPD may require public entities to publish data protection impact assessment reports and suggest administrative measures to address violations.

---

## Chapter V - International Transfer of Data (Articles 33-36)

### Article 33 - Conditions for International Transfer

International transfer of personal data is permitted only in specific situations: to countries or international organizations that provide an adequate degree of data protection as assessed by the ANPD; when the controller demonstrates compliance guarantees through specific contractual clauses, standard contractual clauses, global corporate standards, or regularly issued seals and certificates; when necessary for international legal cooperation; when authorized by the ANPD; when resulting from international cooperation agreements; or for the protection of life or physical safety of the data subject or third party.

### Article 34 - Adequate Level of Protection

The ANPD assesses adequacy based on the destination country's legislation, data nature, compliance with data protection principles, security measures, and judicial and institutional guarantees.

### Article 35 - Cooperation Agreements

The ANPD defines standard contractual clauses and verifies adequacy guarantees for global corporate standards. International cooperation agreements may serve as legal bases for transfer.

### Article 36 - Changes in Adequacy Level

The ANPD may communicate changes in a country's protection level to controllers and may revoke adequacy decisions at any time, requiring controllers to adopt compliance measures.

---

## Chapter VI - Data Processing Agents (Articles 37-45)

### Section I - Controller and Processor (Articles 37-40)

### Article 37 - Records of Processing Activities

Controllers and processors must maintain records of processing operations, including data types, legal bases, purposes, and retention periods, especially when processing is based on legitimate interest.

### Article 38 - Impact Assessment Report

The ANPD may require controllers to prepare a Data Protection Impact Assessment Report (RIPD) describing data types, collection methodology, security measures, and risk analysis with mitigation measures.

### Article 39 - Processor Obligations

Processors must process data strictly according to controller instructions and may not exceed those instructions unless required by law.

### Article 40 - ANPD Standards for Processing Agents

The ANPD may establish standards for high-risk processing activities and provide simplified standards for small and medium-sized enterprises and startups.

### Section II - Data Protection Officer (Article 41)

### Article 41 - Data Protection Officer (DPO)

Controllers must appoint a DPO whose identity must be publicly disclosed. DPO duties include handling data subject complaints, receiving ANPD communications, guiding employees on data protection, and other controller-defined duties. The ANPD may waive the appointment requirement based on entity size or data volume.

### Section III - Liability and Indemnification (Articles 42-45)

### Article 42 - Compensation for Damages

The controller or processor that causes material, moral, individual, or collective damage to others through data processing in violation of data protection legislation is obligated to repair the damage. The processor is jointly liable when it fails to comply with data protection obligations or does not follow the lawful instructions of the controller. Courts may, in civil proceedings, invert the burden of proof in favor of the data subject when the claim is plausible or when evidence production is excessively burdensome.

### Article 43 - Exclusions from Liability

Processing agents are not liable when they prove they did not perform the attributed processing, there was no violation, or the damage resulted exclusively from the data subject's or a third party's fault.

### Article 44 - Irregular Processing

Processing is irregular when it violates legislation or fails to provide reasonably expected security. Processors are liable when they fail to adopt required security measures.

### Article 45 - Consumer Protection Application

Civil liability for data protection violations in consumer relations remains subject to consumer protection legislation, including the Consumer Defense Code.

## Chapter VII - Security and Good Practices (Articles 46-51)

### Section I - Data Security (Articles 46-49)

### Article 46 - Security Measures

Processing agents must adopt technical and administrative security measures to protect data from unauthorized access, destruction, loss, alteration, or unlawful processing. The ANPD may establish minimum technical standards.

### Article 47 - Security by Design

All parties involved in processing must guarantee information security throughout the entire processing lifecycle, from the design phase through conclusion.

### Article 48 - Data Breach Notification

The controller must notify the ANPD and the affected data subjects of security incidents that may create risk or relevant damage within a reasonable time as defined by the ANPD. The notification must include at minimum: the nature of the affected personal data; information about the data subjects involved; the technical and security measures used for protection; the risks related to the incident; the reasons for any delay in communication; and the measures adopted to reverse or mitigate the effects of the incident.

### Article 49 - Information Systems Design

Systems used for personal data processing must be structured to meet security requirements, good practices, and governance principles, incorporating privacy by default and by design.

### Section II - Good Practices and Governance (Articles 50-51)

### Article 50 - Good Practice Programs

Controllers and processors may, individually or through associations, formulate rules of good practice and governance establishing organizational conditions, operating procedures, complaint and petition mechanisms, applicable norms, educational actions, internal oversight mechanisms, and risk mitigation measures. Good practice programs must demonstrate the controller's commitment to data protection, cover all personal data under control, adapt to the structure and scale of operations, establish policies and safeguards based on risk assessment, and create a relationship of trust with data subjects.

### Article 51 - ANPD Encouragement of Standards

The ANPD shall encourage adoption of technical standards facilitating data subject control and promote good practices and governance standards appropriate to specific processing contexts.

---

## Chapter VIII - Enforcement (Articles 52-54)

### Article 52 - Administrative Sanctions

Processing agents that commit infractions are subject to the following ANPD-applied sanctions: a warning with a corrective deadline; a simple fine of up to 2% of the private legal entity's revenue in Brazil in the most recent fiscal year, excluding taxes, capped at R$ 50 million per infraction; a daily fine subject to the same cap; publication of the infraction after investigation; blocking of the affected personal data until regularization; deletion of the affected personal data; partial suspension of database operations for up to six months (renewable); suspension of processing activities for up to six months (renewable); or partial or total prohibition of data processing activities.

### Article 53 - Sanctioning Regulations

The ANPD shall define sanctioning methodologies through regulation after public consultation, establishing circumstances for sanction application, fine calculation methods, and aggravating or mitigating factors.

### Article 54 - Proportionality and Adequacy of Sanctions

Sanctions must be applied after an administrative procedure providing full defense rights, in a gradual, isolated, or cumulative manner according to the specific case. The ANPD must consider the gravity and nature of infractions, good faith of the infractor, economic condition, recidivism, degree of damage, adoption of internal data protection mechanisms and policies, cooperation with the authority, and prompt adoption of corrective measures.

## Chapter IX - National Data Protection Authority (ANPD) (Articles 55-A to 55-L)

### Article 55-A - Establishment of the ANPD

The ANPD is established as a federal public administration body with technical and decision-making autonomy. Its nature is transitory and may be transformed into an independent entity after two years.

### Article 55-B - Legal Nature

The ANPD operates under a special autarchic regime, guaranteeing independence and autonomy in exercising its competencies.

### Article 55-C - Composition

The ANPD comprises: the Board of Directors (highest deliberative body), the National Council for Personal Data Protection and Privacy, Internal Affairs Division, Ombudsman, legal advisory body, and specialized administrative units.

### Article 55-D - Board of Directors

The Board consists of five directors including the Director-President, appointed by the President of the Republic after Senate approval, serving four-year terms. Directors must have distinguished professional records in data protection or related fields.

### Article 55-E - Dismissal of Directors

Directors may only be removed for resignation, final judicial conviction, or disciplinary proceedings. Early dismissal requires a reasoned presidential request approved by an absolute Senate majority.

### Article 55-F - Quarantine Period

Directors face a six-month quarantine after leaving office, barring private-sector data protection activities. They receive full compensation during this period.

### Article 55-G - Conflict of Interest

ANPD directors are subject to the Conflict of Interest Law (Law 12.813/2013) and federal public servant ethical and disciplinary standards.

### Article 55-H - Revenue and Budget

ANPD revenue includes budgetary allocations, donations, sanction proceeds, and amounts from agreements and contracts.

### Article 55-I - ANPD Personnel

ANPD personnel follow the federal civil servants' regime, and the authority may requisition federal servants for temporary assignment.

### Article 55-J - ANPD Competencies

The ANPD is responsible for: ensuring the protection of personal data in accordance with legislation; preparing guidelines for a National Policy on Personal Data Protection and Privacy; overseeing and applying sanctions for data processing violations; promoting awareness and understanding of data protection norms; stimulating adoption of standards that facilitate data subject control; issuing opinions and conducting administrative procedures; promoting international cooperation with foreign data protection authorities; and editing regulations and procedures on data protection and privacy.

### Article 55-K - National Council Composition

The National Council comprises 23 representatives from government, civil society, scientific institutions, and the private sector, serving two-year advisory terms.

### Article 55-L - National Council Competencies

The Council proposes strategic guidelines, prepares annual implementation reports, suggests ANPD actions, conducts studies and public hearings, and disseminates data protection knowledge.

---

## Chapter X - Final and Transitional Provisions (Articles 56-65)

### Article 56 - Transitional Provision for ANPD

The ANPD and National Council must be created within 28 days of the establishing law's publication. Transitional provisions apply until the ANPD is fully structured.

### Article 57 - ANPD Transition Costs

Transitional implementation costs are borne by the Presidency of the Republic's budget.

### Article 58 - Former Provision (Revoked)

Revoked by Provisional Measure 869/2018, which restructured the ANPD's governance model. Superseded by current Chapter IX articles.

### Article 59 - Former Provision (Revoked)

Also revoked by Provisional Measure 869/2018 as part of the ANPD institutional reorganization. Replaced by updated Chapter IX provisions.

### Article 60 - Amendments to Consumer Defense Code

Amends the Consumer Defense Code (Law 8.078/1990) to include personal data protection as a consumer right, linking consumer protection and data protection frameworks.

### Article 61 - Amendments to Civil Registry Law

Amends the Public Records Law to require personal data protection compliance in public registry activities performed under delegated public functions.

### Article 62 - Amendments to Access to Information Law

Requires coordination between the ANPD and access-to-information oversight entities to harmonize data protection and transparency obligations for public entities.

### Article 63 - Existing Rights Preserved

LGPD rights do not exclude other protections from the legal system, including international treaties and conventions to which Brazil is a party.

### Article 64 - Sectoral Rights Preserved

LGPD complements existing Brazilian legislation including the Consumer Defense Code and Marco Civil da Internet (Law 12.965/2014).

### Article 65 - Entry into Force

The law entered into force in stages: ANPD provisions (Articles 55-A through 55-L) on December 28, 2018; administrative sanctions (Articles 52-54) on August 1, 2021; and remaining articles on September 18, 2020. The staggered entry into force allowed organizations time to adapt.
