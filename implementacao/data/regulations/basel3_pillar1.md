## Overview

Basel III is the third major iteration of the international regulatory framework developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), housed within the Bank for International Settlements (BIS). The framework was introduced in response to the 2007-2009 global financial crisis, which exposed critical weaknesses in bank capital adequacy, liquidity management, and risk governance. The Basel III reforms were first published in December 2010 and subsequently revised through a series of amendments, most notably the finalised reforms published in December 2017 under BCBS d424, commonly referred to as "Basel III: Finalising post-crisis reforms."

The evolution from Basel I (1988) to Basel II (2004) to Basel III represents a progressive strengthening of prudential standards. Basel I introduced the concept of risk-weighted assets and an 8% minimum capital ratio. Basel II refined risk measurement by introducing three pillars: minimum capital requirements (Pillar 1), supervisory review (Pillar 2), and market discipline (Pillar 3). Basel III significantly raised the quality and quantity of regulatory capital, introduced macroprudential buffers, established a leverage ratio backstop, and reformed the approaches for measuring credit risk, market risk, and operational risk.

Pillar 1 of Basel III specifies the minimum capital requirements that banks must hold against credit risk, market risk, and operational risk. These requirements are expressed as ratios of regulatory capital to risk-weighted assets (RWA). The objective is to ensure that banks maintain sufficient loss-absorbing capacity to withstand financial stress while continuing to support the real economy. Full implementation of the finalised Basel III standards is scheduled for January 2028, following a phased transition period.

## Capital Definition

Under Basel III, regulatory capital is divided into three tiers based on loss-absorbing quality. Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital represents the highest quality of capital and consists of common shares, retained earnings, accumulated other comprehensive income, and other disclosed reserves. CET1 capital must be fully available to absorb losses on a going-concern basis.

Additional Tier 1 (AT1) capital includes instruments such as contingent convertible bonds (CoCos) and other perpetual non-cumulative instruments that can absorb losses through conversion to equity or write-down upon the occurrence of a trigger event. AT1 instruments must have no maturity date and the issuer must have full discretion to cancel coupon payments.

Tier 2 capital encompasses subordinated debt with an original maturity of at least five years, general loan-loss provisions (up to 1.25% of credit risk-weighted assets under the standardised approach), and other instruments that provide loss absorption in a gone-concern scenario. Tier 2 instruments are subject to amortisation in their final five years before maturity.

Basel III imposes rigorous regulatory adjustments and deductions from capital. These include deductions for goodwill and other intangible assets, deferred tax assets arising from temporary differences, investments in own shares, reciprocal cross-holdings, investments in the capital of banking and financial entities outside the scope of regulatory consolidation, and shortfalls in provisions relative to expected losses under the IRB approach. These deductions are primarily applied to CET1 capital, thereby ensuring that reported capital ratios reflect genuinely loss-absorbing resources.

## Minimum Capital Requirements

The Basel III framework establishes three minimum capital ratios that all internationally active banks must satisfy at all times. The minimum CET1 capital ratio is 4.5% of risk-weighted assets. The minimum Tier 1 capital ratio, which comprises CET1 plus AT1 capital, is 6.0% of risk-weighted assets. The minimum total capital ratio, which includes Tier 1 plus Tier 2 capital, is 8.0% of risk-weighted assets.

These minimum ratios represent a significant increase in both the quantity and quality of capital relative to Basel II. Under Basel II, the minimum Tier 1 ratio was 4.0%, and there was no explicit CET1 requirement. The Basel III CET1 requirement of 4.5% alone exceeds the prior Tier 1 minimum, reflecting the post-crisis consensus that common equity is the primary form of loss absorption.

When combined with the capital conservation buffer of 2.5%, the effective minimum CET1 ratio becomes 7.0%, and the effective minimum total capital ratio becomes 10.5%. Banks that fail to meet the combined buffer requirements face automatic restrictions on discretionary distributions, including dividends, share buybacks, and bonus payments, under the maximum distributable amount (MDA) framework.

## Capital Buffers

The capital conservation buffer (CCB) of 2.5% of RWA, composed entirely of CET1 capital, sits above the minimum capital requirements. Introduced under BCBS d189, its purpose is to ensure that banks accumulate capital during normal economic conditions that can be drawn down during periods of stress. When a bank's CET1 ratio falls within the buffer range, progressively stricter constraints are placed on earnings distributions.

The countercyclical capital buffer (CCyB) ranges from 0% to 2.5% of RWA and is also composed of CET1 capital. National authorities set the CCyB rate for their jurisdiction based on macroeconomic and financial conditions, particularly the credit-to-GDP gap. When excessive credit growth signals the build-up of systemic risk, authorities may activate or increase the buffer. Conversely, the buffer may be released during downturns to encourage continued lending.

Global systemically important banks (G-SIBs) are subject to additional capital surcharges ranging from 1.0% to 3.5% of RWA, depending on the bank's systemic importance score. The G-SIB assessment methodology, described in BCBS d296, evaluates banks across five categories: size, interconnectedness, substitutability, complexity, and cross-jurisdictional activity. As of 2023, no bank has been assigned to the highest surcharge bucket. Domestic systemically important banks (D-SIBs) may be subject to additional surcharges set by national authorities, typically ranging from 0.5% to 2.0%.

## Credit Risk - Standardised Approach

The standardised approach for credit risk assigns risk weights to exposures based on external credit ratings and asset class categories. Under the revised standardised approach published in BCBS d424, sovereign exposures to AAA-rated governments receive a 0% risk weight, while exposures to governments rated below B- receive a 150% risk weight. Unrated sovereigns are assigned risk weights based on national discretion.

Bank exposures are risk-weighted according to the external credit rating of the counterparty bank, with risk weights ranging from 20% for AAA to AA- rated banks, to 150% for banks rated below B-. The revised framework eliminates the option to use the sovereign rating as a proxy for bank risk weights in certain cases, introducing instead a standardised credit risk assessment approach (SCRA) for unrated bank exposures, with risk weights of 40% for Grade A and 75% for Grade B banks.

Corporate exposures receive risk weights from 20% (AAA to AA-) to 150% (below BB-), with unrated corporate exposures generally assigned a 100% risk weight. The revised framework introduces more granular risk weights for specialised lending exposures, including project finance, object finance, and commodity finance, with risk weights ranging from 80% to 130% depending on the phase and quality of the project.

Residential mortgage exposures benefit from preferential risk weights. Under the revised standardised approach, risk weights depend on the loan-to-value (LTV) ratio, ranging from 20% for LTV up to 50%, to 70% for LTV between 90% and 100%. Loans that are materially dependent on cash flows generated by the property are treated more conservatively. Retail exposures that meet specific granularity and diversification criteria are assigned a 75% risk weight.

## Credit Risk - Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) Approach

The IRB approach allows banks to use their internal models to estimate key risk parameters for calculating regulatory capital. Under the Foundation IRB (F-IRB) approach, banks estimate the probability of default (PD) for each borrower, while the loss given default (LGD), exposure at default (EAD), and maturity (M) are prescribed by the supervisor. Under the Advanced IRB (A-IRB) approach, banks estimate all four parameters internally, subject to regulatory floors and supervisory validation.

The risk weight function for corporate, sovereign, and bank exposures under the IRB approach is based on the asymptotic single-risk-factor (ASRF) model. The capital requirement (K) for a given exposure is derived from the conditional PD under a stressed scenario, the LGD, and a maturity adjustment factor. The key formula involves the computation of a correlation parameter (R), which varies by asset class and PD level, and the application of the inverse normal distribution to translate PD into a stressed default probability at the 99.9% confidence level.

The Basel III finalised reforms introduced significant constraints on the use of IRB approaches. As published in BCBS d424, the A-IRB approach is no longer available for exposures to large and mid-sized corporates (consolidated revenues exceeding EUR 500 million), banks, and other financial institutions. For these portfolios, banks must use either the F-IRB approach or the standardised approach. Additionally, input floors have been set for key risk parameters: a PD floor of 5 basis points (0.05%) for corporate exposures, LGD floors of 25% for unsecured corporate exposures and 10% for secured exposures (with variations by collateral type), and an EAD floor equal to the sum of on-balance-sheet exposures and 50% of off-balance-sheet exposures using applicable credit conversion factors.

## Market Risk - Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB)

The Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB), published as BCBS d457 in January 2019 (revised minimum capital requirements for market risk), represents a comprehensive overhaul of the market risk framework. The FRTB introduces a clearer boundary between the trading book and the banking book, reducing opportunities for regulatory arbitrage. Banks must document their intent to trade and meet strict criteria for instruments held in the trading book, with supervisory powers to reassign positions.

The revised standardised approach (SA) under FRTB is based on a sensitivities-based method (SbM) that captures delta, vega, and curvature risks. Risk charges are computed for seven risk classes: general interest rate risk (GIRR), credit spread risk for non-securitisations, credit spread risk for securitisations (CTP and non-CTP), equity risk, commodity risk, and foreign exchange risk. The SA also includes a default risk charge (DRC) and a residual risk add-on (RRAO) for instruments with exotic payoffs.

The internal models approach (IMA) under FRTB replaces value-at-risk (VaR) with expected shortfall (ES) as the primary risk measure, calibrated to a 97.5% confidence level over a variable liquidity horizon. The IMA requires banks to pass both backtesting and profit-and-loss attribution (P&L attribution) tests at the desk level. Desks that fail these tests are reverted to the standardised approach, enforcing discipline in model quality and accuracy.

## Operational Risk - Standardised Measurement Approach (SMA)

Basel III replaces all prior approaches to operational risk capital (including the Basic Indicator Approach, the Standardised Approach, and the Advanced Measurement Approaches) with a single Standardised Measurement Approach (SMA), as specified in BCBS d424. The SMA eliminates the use of internal models for operational risk capital, addressing concerns about the comparability and reliability of modelled operational risk estimates.

The SMA calculates operational risk capital based on two components: the Business Indicator Component (BIC) and the Internal Loss Multiplier (ILM). The Business Indicator (BI) is a financial-statement-based proxy for operational risk exposure, computed as the sum of three components: the interest, leases, and dividends component (ILDC), the services component (SC), and the financial component (FC). The BI is then mapped to a marginal coefficient structure that increases progressively across three buckets, producing the BIC.

The Internal Loss Multiplier incorporates a bank's historical loss experience (average annual operational risk losses over a 10-year period) into the capital calculation. Banks with higher historical losses relative to their BIC will face a multiplier above 1.0, while banks with lower loss experience may benefit from a multiplier below 1.0. National supervisors retain the discretion to set the ILM equal to 1.0 for all banks in their jurisdiction, effectively neutralising the impact of internal loss data.

## Leverage Ratio

The Basel III leverage ratio is a non-risk-based backstop measure designed to constrain the build-up of excessive leverage in the banking system. The minimum leverage ratio is set at 3% of Tier 1 capital to the total leverage exposure measure. G-SIBs are subject to an additional leverage ratio buffer equal to 50% of their risk-weighted G-SIB surcharge, which must be met with CET1 capital.

The leverage exposure measure encompasses on-balance-sheet exposures, derivative exposures (measured using SA-CCR), securities financing transaction (SFT) exposures, and off-balance-sheet items converted using specific credit conversion factors. Unlike the risk-based capital framework, the leverage ratio does not differentiate between exposures based on their riskiness, thereby serving as a safeguard against model risk and risk-weight optimisation.

The leverage ratio was introduced in BCBS d270 (revised in BCBS d424) to address a critical lesson of the financial crisis: several large banks that appeared well-capitalised on a risk-weighted basis had, in fact, built up excessive leverage through on- and off-balance-sheet exposures. The leverage ratio provides a simple, transparent, and comparable measure that complements the risk-based capital requirements.

## Output Floor

The output floor, introduced as a key component of the Basel III finalised reforms in BCBS d424, sets a lower bound on the capital requirements calculated using internal models. Specifically, the aggregate risk-weighted assets calculated using internal models-based approaches cannot be lower than 72.5% of the RWA that would result from applying the revised standardised approaches across all risk categories (credit risk, market risk, and operational risk).

The output floor is designed to address the excessive variability in risk-weighted assets observed across banks using internal models, and to ensure a minimum level of conservatism. The floor is being phased in gradually: 50% from January 2023, increasing by 5 percentage points annually, reaching the full 72.5% by January 2028. During the transition period, national supervisors may apply a cap on the increase in total RWA resulting from the output floor.

The output floor applies at the consolidated level and is calculated as the maximum of (a) total RWA computed using the bank's approved approaches, and (b) 72.5% of total RWA computed using only standardised approaches. This mechanism effectively limits the capital benefit that banks can derive from using internal models, promoting greater consistency and comparability across the banking sector.

## Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA)

Total risk-weighted assets represent the denominator in the capital adequacy ratio calculation and comprise the sum of RWA for credit risk, market risk, and operational risk. For credit risk, RWA equals the exposure amount multiplied by the applicable risk weight under the standardised approach, or is derived from the IRB capital requirement formula (K multiplied by 12.5 multiplied by EAD) under the IRB approach.

Market risk RWA is calculated as 12.5 multiplied by the market risk capital charge derived under either the FRTB standardised approach or the FRTB internal models approach. Operational risk RWA equals 12.5 multiplied by the operational risk capital charge computed under the SMA. The factor of 12.5 (the reciprocal of the 8% minimum total capital ratio) converts capital charges into RWA-equivalent amounts.

The calculation of total RWA is subject to the output floor, which ensures that total RWA does not fall below 72.5% of what would result from exclusive use of standardised approaches. Additionally, credit valuation adjustment (CVA) risk charges, calculated under either the basic approach (BA-CVA) or the standardised approach (SA-CVA) as specified in BCBS d424, contribute to total RWA and capture the risk of mark-to-market losses on derivatives due to deterioration in counterparty creditworthiness.

## Counterparty Credit Risk (SA-CCR)

The Standardised Approach for Counterparty Credit Risk (SA-CCR), introduced in BCBS d279 and incorporated into the Basel III finalised framework, replaces the prior Current Exposure Method (CEM) and Standardised Method (SM) for measuring counterparty credit risk exposures arising from OTC derivatives, exchange-traded derivatives, and long-settlement transactions. SA-CCR provides a more risk-sensitive framework that differentiates between margined and unmargined transactions.

The SA-CCR exposure at default is calculated as: EAD = alpha multiplied by (Replacement Cost + Potential Future Exposure), where alpha is set at 1.4. Replacement Cost (RC) reflects the current mark-to-market exposure, considering the value of collateral and netting agreements. For margined trades, RC also incorporates the minimum transfer amount and the threshold. Potential Future Exposure (PFE) is computed as a multiplier (which reduces PFE when the portfolio is over-collateralised or out-of-the-money) applied to an aggregate add-on across five asset classes: interest rates, foreign exchange, credit, equity, and commodity.

The SA-CCR framework recognises the risk-reducing benefits of netting agreements and margin arrangements more accurately than prior methods. Within each asset class, hedging sets allow for the partial or full offsetting of long and short positions. The framework applies supervisory-determined parameters including supervisory delta adjustments, supervisory volatilities, and supervisory correlation parameters, ensuring consistency and comparability across banks. SA-CCR outputs feed into both the risk-based capital framework (as EAD inputs to credit risk RWA calculations) and the leverage ratio exposure measure.
