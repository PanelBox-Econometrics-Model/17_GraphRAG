# Resolucao BCB 4.658/2018 - Cybersecurity Policy (Superseded)

Resolution 4.658 was enacted by the National Monetary Council (Conselho Monetario Nacional - CMN) on April 26, 2018. It was the first comprehensive regulation issued by the Banco Central do Brasil (BCB) establishing mandatory requirements for cybersecurity policies and for the contracting of cloud computing services by financial institutions authorized to operate in Brazil. This resolution was superseded by Resolution 4.893/2021, which consolidated, updated, and replaced the original framework.

**Status: This resolution has been repealed and is no longer in force. It was superseded by Resolution 4.893/2021, effective July 1, 2021.**

## Scope and Purpose

Resolution 4.658/2018 applied to all financial institutions and other entities authorized to operate by the Banco Central do Brasil, as defined in Article 1. The regulation was enacted in response to the increasing frequency and sophistication of cyber threats targeting the Brazilian financial system and aligned Brazil's regulatory posture with international standards and best practices, including frameworks from the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) and the Financial Stability Board (FSB).

The primary objectives of the resolution were:

- To require financial institutions to establish and maintain a formal cybersecurity policy.
- To set minimum standards for the contracting of cloud computing, data processing, and data storage services.
- To ensure that the BCB maintained supervisory access to data and information processed by third-party service providers.
- To define governance structures and accountability for cybersecurity within financial institutions.

## Original Cybersecurity Policy Requirements

Under Articles 2 through 5, Resolution 4.658/2018 required each institution to develop, implement, and maintain a cybersecurity policy encompassing:

- Objectives for cybersecurity aligned with the institution's strategic plan and risk management framework (Article 2, Paragraph I).
- Procedures and controls to reduce the institution's vulnerability to cybersecurity incidents, covering hardware, software, communication networks, and data assets (Article 2, Paragraph II).
- Controls for the traceability of information, ensuring that actions performed in critical systems could be audited and attributed to individual users (Article 2, Paragraph III).
- Classification of data and information according to relevance and sensitivity, establishing differentiated protection levels for each category (Article 3).
- Mechanisms for incident detection, prevention, and response, including real-time monitoring and threat intelligence capabilities (Article 3, Paragraph IV).
- Awareness programs to promote a cybersecurity culture among employees, contractors, and third-party personnel with access to the institution's systems (Article 4).

The cybersecurity policy was required to be approved by the board of directors or, in the absence of a board, by the executive board of the institution (Article 5). Annual review and update of the policy was mandatory.

## Information Security Standards

Articles 6 and 7 established that institutions must implement information security standards and procedures covering:

- Authentication and access management, including the use of multi-factor authentication for access to critical systems and sensitive data.
- Network security, including segmentation, intrusion detection and prevention systems, and firewall management.
- Cryptographic standards for data at rest and data in transit, with requirements for key management procedures.
- Physical and environmental security for data centers and critical infrastructure.
- Backup and recovery procedures, including regular testing of backup restoration processes.
- Change management procedures to ensure that modifications to systems and infrastructure do not introduce new vulnerabilities.

Institutions were required to document their information security standards and make them available to the BCB upon request (Article 7, Paragraph II).

## Cloud Computing Services Provisions

Resolution 4.658/2018 introduced specific provisions for the contracting of cloud computing services by financial institutions (Articles 9 through 16). These provisions were among the most significant aspects of the regulation and included:

### Prior Communication to BCB

Article 9 required institutions to communicate to the BCB their intention to contract cloud computing services for the processing, storage, or handling of data related to operations conducted in Brazil. This communication was required to include details about the service provider, the nature of the services, and the geographic location of data processing.

### Data Localization and Cross-Border Processing

Article 12 established that data related to operations conducted in Brazil should, as a general rule, be processed and stored within the national territory. Cross-border processing was permitted under specific conditions:

- The institution had to demonstrate that the BCB would maintain effective access to data and information stored abroad.
- Contractual provisions had to guarantee data portability and repatriation in the event of service termination.
- The institution was required to assess the legal and regulatory environment of the destination country to ensure adequate data protection.

### Provider Requirements

Cloud service providers were required to (Article 14):

- Maintain appropriate security certifications and comply with internationally recognized standards.
- Allow audits and inspections by the institution and by the BCB, directly or through appointed third parties.
- Ensure data segregation and implement controls to prevent unauthorized access.
- Provide contractual guarantees regarding service continuity, disaster recovery, and incident notification.

## Incident Reporting Requirements

Article 8 required institutions to establish procedures for the identification, classification, recording, and reporting of cybersecurity incidents. Key requirements included:

- Definition of criteria for classifying incidents by severity and impact.
- Immediate internal communication to the designated director and the board of directors for significant incidents.
- Notification to the BCB of relevant cybersecurity incidents within timeframes established by supplementary regulations.
- Post-incident analysis, including root cause investigation and implementation of corrective and preventive measures.
- Documentation and retention of incident records for a minimum period of five years.

## Director Responsibility and Governance

Article 5 mandated the designation of a specific director responsible for cybersecurity policy implementation and enforcement. This director was required to:

- Hold a position at the executive board level with sufficient authority to enforce cybersecurity decisions across the organization.
- Report directly to the board of directors on cybersecurity matters.
- Ensure that the cybersecurity policy was effectively implemented and that the institution maintained compliance with all applicable regulatory requirements.
- Present an annual report to the board of directors summarizing the institution's cybersecurity posture, incidents that occurred during the period, and actions taken to address identified vulnerabilities.

The board of directors held ultimate responsibility for approving the cybersecurity policy, ensuring the adequacy of resource allocation, and overseeing the institution's cybersecurity risk management (Article 5, Paragraph II).

## Implementation Timeline

Resolution 4.658/2018 established a phased implementation timeline to allow institutions to adapt their operations and governance structures:

- **Within 180 days of enactment (by October 2018):** Institutions were required to have approved and published their cybersecurity policy, designate the responsible director, and establish the incident response framework.
- **Within 360 days of enactment (by April 2019):** Institutions were required to have fully implemented the cybersecurity policy, including awareness programs, technical controls, and governance structures.
- **Cloud computing provisions:** Institutions with existing cloud computing contracts were required to adapt those contracts to comply with the new requirements within the deadlines established for each obligation. New contracts entered into after the resolution's effective date were required to comply from inception.

Institutions classified in the S5 segment (smaller institutions) were granted extended deadlines and permitted to adopt simplified compliance approaches, consistent with the principle of proportionality.

## Relationship with Resolution 4.893/2021

Resolution 4.658/2018 was repealed in its entirety by Resolution 4.893/2021, which entered into force on July 1, 2021. The superseding resolution maintained the core structure and objectives of the original framework while introducing several updates and refinements:

### Key Changes Introduced by Resolution 4.893/2021

- **Consolidation and simplification:** The new resolution reorganized and streamlined the regulatory text, reducing ambiguities and consolidating provisions that had been subject to interpretation during the application of Resolution 4.658/2018.
- **Enhanced incident response requirements:** Resolution 4.893/2021 expanded the requirements for incident response plans, including mandatory simulation exercises and more detailed post-incident analysis procedures.
- **Updated cloud computing provisions:** The rules for contracting cloud computing services were refined to reflect the evolution of cloud technologies and market practices, including clearer guidance on cross-border data processing and provider certification requirements.
- **Strengthened governance framework:** The responsibilities of the board of directors and the designated cybersecurity director were expanded, with more explicit requirements for oversight, reporting, and accountability.
- **Alignment with LGPD:** Resolution 4.893/2021 incorporated explicit references to the Lei Geral de Protecao de Dados (LGPD - Law 13.709/2018), which had not yet been enacted when Resolution 4.658/2018 was issued. The updated resolution established complementary data protection obligations for financial institutions.
- **Improved proportionality provisions:** The new resolution provided more detailed guidance on the application of proportionality for institutions of different sizes and complexity levels, particularly for those in the S4 and S5 segments.

Institutions that had achieved compliance with Resolution 4.658/2018 were considered provisionally compliant with Resolution 4.893/2021, subject to adapting their policies and procedures to the updated requirements within the transition period established by the new resolution.

Understanding Resolution 4.658/2018 remains relevant for historical analysis of the evolution of cybersecurity regulation in the Brazilian financial sector and for interpreting decisions, sanctions, and supervisory actions that were based on the original framework during the period from April 2018 to June 2021.
